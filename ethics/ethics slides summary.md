### **1. Artificial Morality (AMAs)**

- **Definition**: The goal of artificial morality is to design artificial agents to act as if they are moral agents.
- **Top-Down Approach**: This strategy involves turning explicit moral theories (e.g., Utilitarianism, Deontology, or Asimov's Laws) into specific algorithms for an agent to follow.
- **Bottom-Up Approach**: This strategy seeks to provide environments where appropriate behavior is learned, selected, or evolved through experience, similar to a child's moral education.
- **Hybrid Approach**: Because single approaches often fail, a hybrid strategy integrates diverse philosophies and architectures to achieve sophisticated moral sensibility.
- **Evaluation**: The **Moral Turing Test** is a proposed strategy to evaluate whether an artificial agent’s behavior is acceptable compared to human performance.
    

### **2. Epistemology and Knowledge**
- **Standard Definition of Knowledge**: In the JTB theory (Plato), knowledge is defined as **Justified True Belief** .
- **Gettier Problem**: These problems are cases where someone has a justified true belief that fails to count as knowledge because the justification is not formally related to the truth (e.g., the example of the broken clock that happens to show the right time).
- **Descartes’ Rationalism**: René Descartes is famous for the phrase "I think, therefore I exist" (_Je pense, donc je suis_) and for his method of **methodological doubt**, where he rejects anything that can be doubted to find a certain foundation for knowledge.
- **Information vs. Data**: Data are considered pure facts, while information consists of organized data that "makes sense".
### **3. Action Theory (Donald Davidson)**
- **Primary Reason**: Davidson argues that a reason for an action consists of two parts: (a) a **pro-attitude** (a desire, goal, or value) and (b) a **belief** that the action is of a certain kind to satisfy that attitude.
- **Causal Explanation**: Davidson's central thesis is that the primary reason for an action is also its **cause**; thus, rationalizing an action is a species of ordinary causal explanation.
### **4. Normative Ethics**
- **Deontology (Kant)**: This approach focuses on **duty** (_to deon_) and the intrinsic features of an action rather than its consequences.
- **Categorical Imperative**: Kant’s "test" for morality: "Act only on that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law".
- **Perfect vs. Imperfect Duties**: A perfect duty is one where the maxim contradicts itself if universalized (e.g., lying), while an imperfect duty is one that a rational being cannot will as a universal law .
- **Utilitarianism (Bentham & Mill)**: A teleological theory where the morality of an action depends solely on its **consequences** and the maximization of "good" (pleasure or happiness).
- **Act Utilitarianism**: You must choose the action that is most beneficial in every single specific situation.
- **Rule Utilitarianism**: You must follow the rule of action that has the most beneficial consequences when everyone follows it.
- **Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)**: This approach focuses on character and asks, "What kind of person should I be?" rather than just "What should I do?".
- **The Golden Mean**: Aristotle argues that virtues are character traits representing a median point between two vices (excess and deficiency).

### **5. The Mind-Body Problem and Consciousness**
- **Dualism**: The view that the mind (immaterial) and the body (material) are separate entities (Descartes).
- **Monism**: The view that there is only one fundamental substance, such as **Physicalism** (the mind is the brain).
- **Thomas Nagel’s "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"**:
    - **Subjectivity**: Nagel argues that the "subjective character of experience" cannot be reduced to physical brain states. 
- **Point of View**: Conscious experience is inherently tied to a specific point of view, which objective physical theories ("a view from nowhere") necessarily abandon.

### **1. Artificial Morality & Machine Ethics**
This section focuses on how we can program or evolve morality in artificial agents (AMAs).
- **Primary Goal:** To transition from "operational morality" (where the designer is responsible) to "functional morality" (where the agent itself makes moral decisions).
- **The Top-Down Approach:** * **Mechanism:** Applying a pre-defined ethical theory (e.g., Utilitarianism or Kantianism) into an algorithm.    
    - **Examples:** Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics.
    - **Challenge:** The "Frame Problem" (it is difficult to account for every possible context) and the "Conflict Problem" (rules often contradict each other).
- **The Bottom-Up Approach:**
    - **Mechanism:** Agents learn moral behavior through experience, rewards, or evolutionary algorithms, similar to human development    
    - **Techniques:** Genetic algorithms, reinforcement learning, and artificial life.
    - **Challenge:** "Black box" transparency; it is hard to explain _why_ a machine made a specific moral choice if it was learned through training.
- **Hybrid Systems:** The most promising path, combining top-down rules (to provide safety bounds) with bottom-up learning (to handle real-world nuance).
- **The Moral Turing Test (MTT):** A variant of the Turing Test where a machine is considered "moral" if its responses to moral dilemmas are indistinguishable from those of a human moral agent.

### **2. Epistemology: The Study of Knowledge**
Exam questions in this area often focus on the definitions of knowledge and the relationship between information and truth.
- **The Standard Analysis (JTB):** Knowledge = Justified + True + Belief.
    - If any one of these is missing, it is not "Knowledge" in the classical sense.
- **Gettier Cases:** These are "counter-examples" that prove JTB is insufficient. They describe situations where a belief is true and justified, but the truth is actually a result of **luck**, not the justification (e.g., seeing a fake sheep in a field and concluding there is a sheep, while a real sheep happens to be hidden behind a bush).
- **Information Hierarchy:** * **Data:** Unorganized symbols or signals.
    - **Information:** Data that is well-formed and meaningful (semantic).
    - **Knowledge:** Information that has been processed, validated, and integrated into a belief system.

### **3. Action Theory (Donald Davidson)**

This section bridges the gap between psychology (reasons) and physics (causes).

- **Pro-Attitudes:** These include desires, urges, moral views, and aesthetic principles. They are the "motivating" force behind an action.
    
- **Primary Reasons:** An action is explained by a "primary reason," which consists of a **Pro-attitude** toward a goal and a **Belief** that the action will achieve that goal.
    
- **Causal Thesis:** Davidson’s most famous claim is that **Reasons are Causes**. When we say "He flipped the switch because he wanted light," the "wanting" is the physical cause of the "flipping."
    
- **Intentionality:** For an event to be an "action," it must be intentional under some description.
